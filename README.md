Charon — the bone xylophone of the ferryman. here's what's in it:
The background is the River Styx itself — slow dark water with surface ripples barely visible, and mist wisps drifting horizontally across the screen in layers, each one a gradient that fades at the edges like real fog. Faint souls float in the upper void like stars that aren't quite stars.
The instrument is a full SVG xylophone frame built from driftwood rails with ball-joint ends and rope lashings. Thirteen bone bars, each one shaped like a real xylophone bar — the hourglass waist cut that tuners use. Every bar has longitudinal grain texture lines, a marrow hole at the center (the hollow ellipse that makes bone sound like bone), and a struck-surface highlight at the top. They graduate from wide and tall on the bass end to narrow and short on the treble end. A floating SVG mallet appears at whatever bar you hit.
The sound is real bone physics — a hard mallet-impact transient, the primary partial, a slightly inharmonic second partial (bone isn't perfectly elastic), a fast-dying third partial, and the hollow sub-resonance from the marrow cavity. Four bone materials change the character: Femur (the standard, balanced), Skull (rounder, more chamber resonance, darker), Rib (thin and bright, quick), Ancient (dry dead bone, almost wood, barely any ring).
Each pitch zone maps to one of the five rivers of the underworld — the header and display update as you move across the bars: Styx, Acheron, Lethe, Phlegethon, Cocytus.
Four scales: Chromatic, Pentatonic, Phrygian, and Styx — an invented dark octatonic scale that doesn't exist anywhere else.
